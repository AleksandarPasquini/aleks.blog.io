Hello Internet! Today I am going to follow up my first experiment. My last experiment studied the summarisation abilities of GTP-4 vs Humans but the evaluation what does done by me. This is obviously biased so instead I am now going to use more “objective” metrics So what metrics can I use? I am just going to focus on the two most common ones: BLEU and ROUGE.

BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) metric is a method for mainly evaluating the quality of text which has been machine-translated from one natural language to another. However, this can be extended to summaries. The core idea is to measure how many N-grams (a sequence of N words) in the machine translation match those in the reference translations. This idea of comparing a candidate text to one or more reference texts is applicable to summarisation as well. The BLEU forumla is as follows:

\[ \text{BLEU} = \text{BP} * \exp(\sum_{n=1}^{N} w_n \log (p_n)) \]

Where:

\[ \text{BP} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } c > r \\ \exp(1 - \frac{r}{c}) & \text{if } c \leq r \end{cases} \] \[ \text{p}_n = \frac{\text{Number of matching n-grams in machine translation}}{\text{Total number of n-grams in machine translation}} \] \[ c = \text{Length of the machine-translated text} \] \[ r = \text{Reference corpus length} \] \[ w_n = \text{The weight associated with the nth gram precision} \]

ROUGE, which stands for Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation, is a set of metrics used for evaluating automatic summarization of texts as well as machine translation. ROUGE includes series different metrics but today we will just focus on ROUGE-N. ROUGE-N measures the overlap of N-grams between the computer-generated summary and the reference summaries. For example, ROUGE-1 refers to the overlap of individual words, ROUGE-2 refers to the overlap of two-word phrases, and so on. The forumla for ROUGE-1 is shown below:

\[ \text{Recall} = \frac{\text{Sum of matched n-grams from the generated summary}}{\text{Total n-grams in the reference summary}} \] \[ \text{Precision} = \frac{\text{Sum of matched n-grams from the generated summary}}{\text{Total n-grams in the generated summary}} \] \[ \text{F1} = \frac{2*\text{Precision}*\text{Recall}}{\text{Precision}*\text{Recall}} \]

While both BLEU and ROUGE count matching n-grams, BLEU is more precision-oriented, assessing how much of the generated text is correct, while ROUGE is recall-oriented, evaluating how much of the reference text is covered by the generated text.

I will now test and see how well my and Scholar AI’s summary actually summarise. To do this I will use the conclusion from the The rise of traffic classification in IoT networks: A survey as the reference summary.

from rouge import Rouge
from nltk.translate.bleu_score import sentence_bleu

reference = "This paper surveyed recent works in the field of traffic classification in the IoT domain. With the emergence of the IoT concept, various types of devices are being connected to each other. ..."
my_summary = "Tahaei et al (2020) surveyed different traffic classifications for IoT devices. ..."
ai_summary =  "The paper titled The rise of traffic classification in IoT networks: A survey by Hamid Tahaei et al. ..."

print('BLEU score: {}'.format(sentence_bleu(reference.split(), my_summary.split())))
print('BLEU score: {}'.format(sentence_bleu(reference.split(), ai_summary.split())))
print('BLEU score: {}'.format(sentence_bleu(my_summary.split(), ai_summary.split())))

rouge = Rouge()

print(rouge.get_scores(reference, my_summary))
print(rouge.get_scores(reference, ai_summary))
print(rouge.get_scores(my_summary, ai_summary))

Interestingly, there are no overlapping 2,3,4-grams. This causes the BLEU score to go to 0. Additionally, the rouge-1 is slight higher but the scores are still close to zero. This highlights the main flaw with BLEU and ROUGE. Thier reliance on overlapping n-grams means they focus on surface-level lexical similarities rather than the deeper conceptual understanding or coherence of the text. This approach can be problematic for summaries, where the essence is to distill complex information into concise, comprehensible forms while retaining the core meaning and context. They overlook aspects such as whether the summary captures the nuances of the original text, maintains logical coherence, or presents the information in an easily digestible manner.
